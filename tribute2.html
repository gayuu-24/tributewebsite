Education

Palm Beach County Fair (1919):

Augusta Savage's artistic journey began when she was awarded a $25 prize and ribbon for her clay modeling at the fair. This recognition sparked her career in sculpture.

New York City (1921):

Savage moved to New York with a letter of recommendation and little money. She initially sought to study under sculptor Solon Borglum but couldn't afford tuition at the School of American Sculpture. Borglum advised her to apply to Cooper Union, a tuition-free school, where she was admitted, beating out 142 male applicants on the waiting list. Due to her exceptional talent, Cooper Union provided her additional financial support for room and board.

Cooper Union (1921-1923):

Savage studied under sculptor George Brewster and completed the four-year program in just three years.

Further Studies in France (1929):

After years of struggle and support from organizations like the Urban League and the Rosenwald Foundation, Savage studied in Paris at the Académie de la Grande Chaumière. She also worked in the studio of professor Félix Benneteau and studied with sculptor Charles Despiau, exhibiting her work in Paris and winning awards.

Early career

Early Commissions:

After completing her studies, Savage worked in Manhattan and received her first major commission from the New York Public Library to sculpt a bust of W.E.B. Du Bois. This success led to other commissions, including busts of prominent African-American figures like Marcus Garvey and William Pickens Sr.

Racial Discrimination Incident (1923):

Savage applied for a summer program at the Fontainebleau School of Fine Arts but was rejected due to her race after being initially accepted. This incident marked the beginning of her public fight for equal rights in the arts.

Paris Years (1929-1930):

Savage finally realized her dream of studying in France, where she gained international recognition. In Paris, she exhibited her work and toured Europe to study sculpture in various museums and cathedrals. Though she faced some resistance from mentors regarding creative independence, she continued to excel on her own, winning multiple awards at the Paris Salon.
